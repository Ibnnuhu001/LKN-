Why Your CV Gets Rejected Before Anyone Reads It
Most CVs get rejected before a human reads them. Here's why: Companies use ATS software — Applicant Tracking Systems — to automatically filter applications before they reach a recruiter. If your CV isn't formatted correctly, it gets binned. Even if you're perfectly qualified. 3 things that make ATS reject your CV instantly: 1. Tables and text boxes — the scanner can't read them 2. Graphics, icons, and photos — same problem 3. Missing keywords from the job description Fix those three things and you'll already be ahead of 60% of applicants. The job market is competitive enough. Don't let bad formatting be the reason you lose. Save this. Share it with someone who's been applying with no results.

The 6-Second Rule
Recruiters spend an average of 6 seconds on a CV before deciding to read further or move on. 6 seconds. In that window, they're scanning for three things: 1. Your current or most recent job title 2. The company you work/worked for 3. Whether the layout is clean enough to trust That's it. That's the whole first impression. This is why the top third of your CV is the most important section. It needs to earn their attention immediately. How to win those 6 seconds: — Lead with a sharp 3-line professional summary — Make your most recent job title prominent and clear — Use clean formatting with no clutter above the fold If the top of your CV doesn't hook them, nothing below it gets read. What does the top third of your CV look like right now?

5 CV Mistakes That Cost People Jobs
I've reviewed hundreds of CVs. The same five mistakes come up again and again: 1. No professional summary The top of the CV is blank space or a generic objective statement. You have 6 seconds — use them to tell me exactly who you are. 2. Responsibilities instead of achievements "Managed social media accounts" tells me nothing. "Grew Instagram engagement by 78% in 6 months" tells me everything. 3. One CV for every job Tailoring your CV to each role is not optional. A generic CV gets generic results. 4. Wrong file format Sending a CV as a Pages file or with a creative template full of columns and icons. ATS software cannot read them. 5. Too long or too short Two pages is the standard for experienced professionals. One page for graduates. Three pages for nobody. Fix these five things before your next application. Which of these have you been guilty of? Be honest.

How to Write a CV Summary That Gets Read
Most CV summaries are useless. "I am a hardworking, motivated professional seeking a challenging role where I can utilise my skills." That sentence says nothing. It could apply to literally anyone. A strong CV summary does three things in three lines: Line 1 — Who you are professionally (title + years of experience + core speciality) Line 2 — Your biggest career proof point (a result, achievement, or scale of work) Line 3 — What you're seeking and what you bring to it Example: "Results-driven Marketing Manager with 7 years of experience in B2B and consumer brand strategy. Led campaigns generating 2.4M impressions and managed a £1.2M annual budget with 34% improvement in ROAS. Seeking a senior role where I can build and scale high-performing marketing teams." That's specific. That's confident. That earns the next 6 seconds of a recruiter's attention. Try rewriting yours using that structure. What does yours say right now? Drop it in the comments — I'll give you honest feedback.
